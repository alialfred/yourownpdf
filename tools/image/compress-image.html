How to Use Compress Image on YourOwnPDF.com

Large image files slow down websites, clog email attachments, and eat up storage space. Whether you’re a blogger optimising site speed, a photographer sending proofs, or a business professional filing documents, compressing your images is the quickest way to shrink file size without visible quality loss. Use YourOwnPDF.com’s free online image compressor — no sign-up, no uploads, no watermarks. All processing runs privately in your browser; your files never leave your device.

Upload Screen

Step 1: Upload Your Image — Drag & Drop or Browse

Click the upload area or drag an image directly from your computer and drop it into the dotted box. Our tool supports JPG, PNG, WebP, and GIF formats up to 85 MB.

Once loaded, you’ll see a live preview of your original image together with its current file size — so you always know exactly what you’re working with before you compress. If you chose the wrong file, click the × button on the upload icon to remove it and select a different image.

Your image stays private — everything runs locally in your browser. No server uploads.

Compression Settings

Step 2: Tune Your Compression Settings — Level, Format & Method

Three simple controls let you balance file size against image quality:

  • Compression Level — Low (~25%) preserves near-original quality; Medium (~50%) is the best everyday balance; High (~75%) delivers maximum size reduction for emails and web use.
  • Output Format — JPG is ideal for photos and complex gradients. PNG preserves transparency and sharp edges for logos and graphics. WebP offers the best modern compression — supported by Chrome, Firefox, Edge, and Safari.
  • Compression Method — Standard uses fast canvas re-encoding. Smart uses the compressorjs library for a better quality-to-size ratio on complex images.

Tip: use JPG output for photos and WebP for web graphics. PNG only when you need transparency.

Progress Bar

Step 3: Compress & Monitor Real-Time Progress

Click the Compress button to begin. A real-time progress bar shows exactly where we are in the process — from re-encoding pixels to generating the final output. Our tool automatically applies your chosen settings so you get the smallest possible file at the quality you selected.

Most images are processed in under a second. Larger photos may take a few seconds.

Download Result

Step 4: Download Your Optimised Image & Check the Savings

When compression finishes, a results pop-up shows your original size, the new compressed size, and the percentage saved. Click Download to save instantly, or press OK and use the download button below the progress bar.

The compressed file keeps the same dimensions and visual quality but takes up much less space — perfect for websites, email attachments, cloud storage, and social media uploads.

Your original image is never modified. The compressed version is a brand-new file — download it with one click.

Why compress images? Uncompressed images are the #1 cause of slow-loading web pages. A single 5 MB photo can take seconds to load on mobile networks. Compressing removes redundant data, optimises colour data, and resizes pixel dimensions so the file is dramatically smaller while looking nearly identical to the original. YourOwnPDF.com gives you a free, private, browser-based image compressor — no software install, no sign-up, no data leaving your computer.

How much can you compress an image? Our tool typically reduces file size by 25–75% depending on the compression level and image content. Photos with lots of detail compress well at Medium or High settings. Simple graphics and screenshots see strong savings even at Low compression. The actual result appears instantly in the results pop-up — so you always know exactly what you achieved.

Does image compression reduce quality? Yes, but modern compression algorithms are remarkably good at hiding quality loss. At Low and Medium settings the difference is barely visible to the naked eye. At High compression you may notice slight softening in fine details — perfect for web use, thumbnails, and previews where small file size matters more than pixel-perfect fidelity.
